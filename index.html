Abstract

The capacity to represent emotion is a cornerstone of human intelligence. While recent Large Language Models (LLMs) and Multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) show remarkable cognitive abilities, it is unknown whether they can develop genuine, human-like affective representations. Here, we investigate this by comparing the emergent affective representations of LLMs/MLLMs to human behavioral ratings and neural activity in response to 2,180 emotionally evocative short videos. Using millions of triplet odd-one-out judgments, we derived 30-dimensional embeddings from both an MLLM and a language-only model (LLM). We found that these embeddings are highly interpretable, often blended, and organizing emotion primarily along categorical lines. Most remarkably, the MLLM's representation predicted neural activity with the highest accuracy, outperforming both the LLM and, counterintuitively, representations derived from human behavioral ratings. This superior neural alignment was particularly evident in both subcortical (e.g., amygdala, hippocampus, putamen, etc.) and cortical (temporal pole, OFC, PC, TPJ, etc.) emotion-processing regions. These findings suggest that similarity judgment based machine-behavioral paradigms can more faithfully capture the brain's affective representational geometry than traditional rating scales, and provide compelling evidence that MLLMs can autonomously develop rich, brain-aligned conceptual frameworks for emotion, offering a powerful new tool to explore the underpinnings of affective cognition.

Overview of our work.

Schematic diagrams of the experiment and analysis methods.

a, Emotionally evocative video stimuli database. Standardized annotations include: human-rated emotional discrete categories and affective dimensions, detailed video descriptions, and synchronized fMRI recordings during stimulus viewing. b-d, Pipelines of affective embedding learning under the triplet odd-one-out paradigm for human, LLM, and MLLM, respectively. Odd-one-out judgments were collected for approximately 7.1 million triplets, and modeled using the SPoSE approach to derive the corresponding latent embeddings. For humans, the judgments were computed based on the cosine similarity between pairwise ratings of categories or dimensions. e, Examples of prompts and responses for LLM and MLLM. f, Illustration of the SPoSE modeling approach. g, Illustration of the comparison between different representational spaces.

Latent affective representations exhibit stability and predictability in models.

Validation of the latent embeddings learned from similarity judgments over millions of trials.

a, Dimensionality effects on prediction accuracy for measured similarity matrices. b-e, Reproducibility analysis of the 30 recovered dimensions. Each data point indicates the maximum correlation between individual sorted dimensions and all dimensions from independent runs, with shaded regions representing 95% confidence intervals (CI) derived from 10 randomized repetitions. f, Odd-one-out prediction accuracy on models' held-out test sets. Performance metrics display 95% CIs, while noise ceilings - calculated from supplemental behavioral data for each model - indicate mean inter-trial reliability across 10,000 triplets. g-j, RSMs for 66 video stimuli derived through the recovered embeddings and the behavioral validation. Left panel displays embedding-based similarity estimates, while middle panel shows full triplet sampling results from behavioral experiments (similarity operationalized as co-selection frequency across trials). Right panel presents correlation coefficients between predicted and measured pairwise similarities.

Verifying Causal Interpretability of Affective Embeddings through Dimensional Manipulation.

Modifying the affective content of videos through dimensional manipulation of affective embeddings.

a, Our manipulation of specific emotional experiences was achieved by reducing the activation values along targeted dimensions in the affective embeddings (SPoSE embeddings). The first row displays frames from the original video. In the second row, red bounding boxes highlight the target dimension to be manipulated, with its corresponding label and original activation value (normalized to [0,1]) annotated above. The third row presents the activation heatmap for this dimension. The fourth row shows video frames after decreasing the original activation value to 0.2, demonstrating that precisely the regions highlighted in the heatmap were modified. b, The corresponding emotional experience can be elicited in videos by augmenting specific dimension values in the affective embeddings. The subfigure displays the edited video frames obtained by increasing the value of a specific dimension in the affective embedding to 0.8. Note: Due to space constraints, only the dimension label with the highest PCC is shown in the figure. For complete labels, please refer to the main text.

Comparative analysis of affective representations in models and humans.

Analysis of affective space dimensions and clusters.

a-d, Affective graphs (left) and clusters (right). In the affective graphs, nodes represent dimensions of the affective space, with edge widths scaled according to PCC (threshold = 0.2). Isolated nodes are omitted. For emotion clusters, the Louvain algorithm was applied, and nodes belonging to the same cluster are color-coded identically (see legend, top right). Isolated nodes are excluded. Node sizes represent relative PageRank scores. e, Visualization of shared and non-shared clusters between different models. f, To achieve 95%--99% behavioral predictability, the required number of dimensions is shown. Red dashed lines indicate chance-level odd-one-out accuracy, gray bands mark the target predictability range (95%--99%), and colored bars represent dimensional contributions within this range.

Which factors govern human-like affective alignment in models?

A comparative analysis of behavioral alignment between 22 AI models and human responses on an emotional odd-one-out task.

The models span diverse architectures (e.g., DNNs, LLaMA series, Qwen series, DeepSeek series, and ChatGPT series), parameter scales (ranging from 25M to 671B), and training data conditions (e.g., fine-tuned on empathy datasets). Bar plots represent Pearson’s r between human-measured and model-measured RSMs. Slash-shaded bars indicate similarity computed against human category ratings, while solid bars denote similarity with human dimension ratings. Error bars reflect 95% CIs.
